About the Data

Transparency around data sources, scope, and limitations.

What this repository contains

The HBCU Research Repository is a curated, searchable collection of scholarly works affiliated with Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCUs). The goal is to improve discoverability, citation, and visibility of HBCU-affiliated scholarship.

The repository currently includes academic works such as:

  • Journal articles
  • Conference papers
  • Working papers and reports (where available)
  • Open access links to full-text PDFs when provided by the source

Data sources

The data is aggregated from publicly available scholarly metadata sources, including open research indexes and open access discovery services. These sources collect metadata from publishers, repositories, and institutional systems.

HBCU affiliation is determined based on institutional metadata provided by these sources. This may include author affiliations, institutional identifiers, or repository associations.

What “HBCU-affiliated” means

A work is considered HBCU-affiliated if at least one author is associated with an HBCU according to the available metadata at the time of ingestion.

Because institutional metadata is not always complete or consistently reported, some HBCU-affiliated works may be missing, misclassified, or attributed to broader institutions. Improving this accuracy is an ongoing focus of the project.

Data limitations

This repository is an evolving dataset and should not be treated as a complete or authoritative record. Known limitations include:

  • Incomplete or inconsistent institutional affiliations
  • Missing abstracts, keywords, or subject classifications
  • Gaps in open access coverage, even when a work exists publicly
  • Delayed updates as new works are published or indexed

These gaps reflect broader structural issues in academic metadata and discovery systems, not the absence of scholarship.

How the data is updated

The dataset is periodically refreshed and refined as new metadata becomes available and as issues are identified through user feedback. Priority is given to improving:

  • Institutional accuracy
  • Topic and subject consistency
  • Open access links
  • Citation quality

How you can help

If you notice missing works, incorrect affiliations, broken links, or metadata issues, please use the Feedback button on the site.

Contributions from researchers, librarians, and institutional partners are especially valuable in improving coverage and accuracy.
